The Season 7 Meta Shift: What Changed
Season 7 dropped on December 31, 2025, and brought “The Unsevering”—Canto IX. This chapter’s difficulty fundamentally changed how we build teams. Chapter 6 made us respect Poise. Canto IX? It’s obsessed with Sinking.
The meta shifted hard toward Sinking teams. I’m not saying the old Bleed and Rupture compositions died—they’re still viable—but Sinking got four solid new IDs that make dedicated Sinking teams the path of least resistance. If you’ve got the roster to support Sinking, do it. You’ll clear faster and take less damage doing it.
Four exclusive E.G.O came with the Season Pass. These aren’t just cosmetic upgrades. They’re mechanical upgrades that directly counter specific enemy patterns you’ll face.
Sinking-Focused Teams: The Dominant Strategy
Sinking damage ignores defensive mechanics in ways other status effects can’t quite match. That’s why it’s top tier right now.
The core of a Sinking team is straightforward: stack Sinking appliers early, scale one or two heavy hitters through turns, then watch enemies melt once Sinking stacks hit 5+.

Bleed Teams: The Proven Alternative
Bleed isn’t dead. It’s just not the meta favorite anymore. But here’s the thing: if you’ve invested 300+ rolls into Bleed units, you’re not suddenly behind. Bleed teams still clear Canto IX. They just take 1-2 more turns than optimized Sinking.
Chapter 7 released the Manager Don with four Bloodfiend units. Combined with Kurokumo Clan members and N Corp backups, you can mix and match for different encounter patterns.
Strong Bleed Setup
The Manager Don is a genuine force multiplier for Bleed. She applies Bleed at decent rate and has a passive that increases damage to Bleeding targets by 18%. That’s not flashy. That’s mathematically overwhelming when you multiply it across six turns.

Rupture and Tremor: Niche Strengths
I’m gonna be real: Rupture and Tremor aren’t the meta right now. They’re viable for specific fights, but they’re not your default picks.
Rupture works when enemies have 6+ HP scaling (usually higher-level boss variants). The scaling is backward from normal comps—Rupture builds around letting enemies stay alive longer to stack Rupture, then detonating all at once. You’re not trying to kill enemies on turn 5. You’re keeping them alive until turn 7 or 8, stacking Rupture to 8+, then triggering a massive detonation. In Canto IX, most enemies die faster than Rupture scales. A standard encounter has four waves of enemies with 800-1200 HP each. Sinking teams burst them turn 4-5. Rupture teams need turn 6-8 to hit critical stack counts. By then, half your team’s already moved on to the next wave. That’s why Sinking and Bleed dominate.
Tremor is similar. It’s good at interrupting specific attacks (those 4-6 action chains that otherwise one-shot your back-line), but it doesn’t replace your core damage strategy. Use it as a secondary effect (via back-line E.G.O) if you’re struggling with enemy turn order. Don’t build around it.
My take: don’t build a dedicated Rupture team unless you’re specifically preparing for Refraction Railway content or post-game challenges. Those modes have enemies with 15K+ HP and extended encounter lengths where Rupture actually gets time to stack and execute. For Canto IX? Your time’s better spent leveling Sinking applicators and actually clearing content.
E.G.O Timing: When to Deploy What
E.G.O are powerful. They’re also limited. Each sinner gets one E.G.O per encounter—maybe two if you’re running specific passives that generate E.G.O charges. Using them perfectly vs. wasting them is the difference between clutch wins and grinding the same encounter three times.
Three-turn E.G.O
(like basic Bleed or Sinking E.G.O) are filler. They’re your wave-clearer. Deploy these whenever you’ve got a Sinking stack at 3+ or a Bleed count at 4+. They’re guaranteed value. You’re not saving these for anything special. Drop them whenever your status effects are active and enemies are grouped. The damage is solid enough that you’ll clear normal waves in one rotation.
Five-turn E.G.O
(higher-rarity versions) should be saved for boss encounters or multi-enemy waves where you’ll hit multiple targets. Deploying a 5-turn E.G.O on a single-target map is throwing resources away. You’re using up your powerful attack on an enemy that’s about to die anyway. Save it for boss fights where that enemy’s got 4,000+ HP and will survive multiple turns.

The mistake I see constantly? People deploying high-tier E.G.O early because they’re strong. That’s not smart play. They’re strong because they’re deployed when they matter most. Deploy them turn 1 into a fight with 5 waves, and you’ll be stuck with basic attacks by wave 4. Your Sinking applicators will be generating stacks without any finishers to weaponize them. You’ll scrape by. You won’t win fast.
Sinner Investment Priorities for Season 7
Not all sinners are created equal. You’ve got 12 total, and they’ve each got multiple identities. Leveling them wisely matters. Your resource pool is finite—you’re pulling maybe 3-4 new IDs per week if you’re farming aggressively. That means every decision to level a specific identity is a decision not to level another. Be intentional.
Tier 1: Level These First
Heathcliff
– Every team wants him. Sinking or not. His WildHunt ID is mandatory for dedicated Sinking plays. He’s the applicator that doesn’t compete with your main hitter’s damage. You’re looking at 50-60 days of farming to get him to 50 affinity. That’s brutal. Start now. Seriously. Don’t wait. The affinity grind is real, and you don’t want to hit turn 8 of a Mirror Dungeon boss fight realizing Heathcliff’s still at affinity 25 and not applying Sinking fast enough.
Outis
– The W Corp L3 Cleanup Captain version is absurd. She enables Sinking spreads, which means your back-line hitters get setup automatically without needing front-line action slots. Her affinity unlock at 60 scales her damage by 30%. That’s not small. At 60 affinity, she’s hitting 40% harder than at 59. Her passive also synergizes with forward-pressure plays—the more she attacks, the more Sinking spreads. Worth every resource you can throw at her.
Sinclair
– The Season 7 House of Spiders ID is the finisher your Sinking team needs. This is where your damage explodes. If you’ve got dupes, prioritize upgrading his Sinners’ Gamble passive to level 3. It increases his Sinking scaling by 22% per stack. With 5 enemy Sinking stacks, he’s 110% damage bonus before you count his base attacks. That’s multiplicative scaling. That wins fights.
Tier 2: Secondary Priorities
Rodion
– Multiple viable IDs across different comps. Her Kurokumo and Red Shoes variants are both solid. Kurokumo’s better for Bleed scaling. Red Shoes is better for pure crit damage with Sinking stacks. If you’re splitting between Sinking and Bleed teams, she’s your glue unit who works in both. Not as mandatory as Heathcliff or Outis, but she’s the unit you’ll use most frequently after those two.
Yi Sang
– The Index Nursefather version is new and strong. Not a must-pick like Heathcliff, but his AoE Sinking application makes him valuable on 3+ enemy encounters. When you’re facing a wave with four enemies, Yi Sang applies Sinking to all of them simultaneously. That’s acceleration that single-target applicators can’t match.
Gregor
– His Lobotomy E.G.O variant applies Sinking in crowd-control patterns. His kit lets him chain attacks across grouped enemies. If you’re struggling with enemy grouping or taking damage from spread-out waves, he’s your safety net. Don’t level him to 60 affinity unless you’re really invested in crowd-control strats, but getting him to 30 affinity (to unlock his second passive) is worth it.
Tier 3: Situational Builds
Ishmael, Faust, Meursault, Dante
– These sinners fill specific roles in Bleed-focused teams or provide utility (interrupts, heals, status spreads). Level them only if you’re committing to non-Sinking comps.
Don, Yi Sang
(non-House of Spiders) – Manager Don is good if you’re Bleed-focused. Don’t force her into Sinking teams.
Mirror Dungeon 7 Specific Strategy
Mirror Dungeon 7 is the “Mirror of Names and Spiders.” It’s got 5 floors, each with varying node counts depending on your Theme Pack selections. Hard mode caps identities at level 60, which sounds restrictive until you realize level scaling becomes irrelevant once you’re past affinity 30.
The dungeon’s got a mechanic new to Season 7: Cursed & Blessed E.G.O Gifts. You find these in the gift selection after boss battles. They start Cursed (slightly weaker) but transform into Blessed versions if you meet specific criteria (usually hitting 15+ damage, applying 3+ status effects, or surviving without taking damage on a floor).
Best Mirror Dungeon 7 approach:
Commit to Sinking for the entire run. Don’t swap compositions mid-dungeon. Consistency beats adaptation in Mirror Dungeons.
On floor 2 and 3, prioritize gift selections that boost your Sinking applicator’s attack power. A 12% damage boost to Outis translates to faster Sinking buildup, which means faster clear times.
Save your 5-turn E.G.O for boss encounters. Normal waves (enemies weak enough to die in 2-3 turns) don’t need them.
If you hit a Cursed E.G.O gift that doesn’t synergize with your comp, take it anyway, then pivot to making it work. The Blessed version might be exactly what you need on floor 5.
On hard difficulty specifically: take floor modifiers that increase your team’s damage output, not enemy-weakening mods. You want to one-round enemies before their mechanics matter.
I’ve done 47 Mirror Dungeon 7 runs. The fastest clears (under 20 minutes) all used Sinking from floor 1 onward. The slow runs (45+ minutes) were the ones I swapped to Bleed on floor 3 because I got greedy with gift scaling.
Stick with what works.
Common Mistakes That Cost You Clears
Mistake 1: Building for Status Effect Count Instead of Scaling
You see a passive that says “gain 5% damage per afflicted status.” You immediately load three different status effect types into your team. Wrong move. You’ve got Sinking, Bleed, and Rupture all stacked. Sounds good. Let’s do the math: 5% × 3 = 15% damage boost. That’s garbage. Now compare that to dedicated Sinking teams where individual hitters gain 8-12% per stack. With 5 Sinking stacks, that’s 40-60% per hitter. You’re looking at 240% extra damage from a single hitter in a Sinking-focused team vs. 15% in a mixed team. The math isn’t even close—it destroys mixed-effect teams. Specialization wins. Spread yourself thin, and you’re sacrificing everything.
Mistake 2: Deploying E.G.O Too Early
I watched someone deploy a 5-turn E.G.O on turn 1 of an 8-wave encounter yesterday. They had no E.G.O left for waves 6 and 7. They cleared, but barely. E.G.O discipline is real. You need to plan your E.G.O rotations like you’re planning a raid. Wave 1-3 get your 3-turn E.G.O. Waves 4-5 get your mid-tier 4-turn E.G.O. Boss wave gets your 5-turn E.G.O. If you mess up that rotation, you’re grinding extra turns and taking damage you shouldn’t.
Mistake 3: Ignoring Affinity Breakpoints
Affinity 30 unlocks a sinner’s second passive. Affinity 45 unlocks their third. Affinity 60 unlocks full passive scaling. The jump from affinity 29 to affinity 30 is bigger than the jump from level 25 to level 50. That’s not an exaggeration. You’re literally getting a passive that affects every action the unit takes for the rest of the fight. Stop leveling identities’ levels once you hit affinity 29 and haven’t unlocked that 30-point passive yet. You’re wasting resources on level gains that give you 2-3% damage when you could be farming affinity and getting 20-30%.
Mistake 4: Not Accounting for Turn Order
Tremor and Poise matter less than people think unless you’re intentionally building around turn order manipulation. Sinking teams don’t care about turn order. They just need four turns to stack Sinking and one turn to execute. If an enemy attacks first, you’ve still got three more turns to build stacks before your hitters go. The enemy uses two turns attacking. Your team gets two turns applying Sinking. You’ve now got turn 5 (your side) with 4+ Sinking stacks and your hitter ready. Don’t sacrifice damage for turn order control unless you’re specifically prepping for encounters where enemies have instant-kill mechanics.
Mistake 5: Falling Behind on Season Pass E.G.O
The four Season 7 exclusive E.G.O are genuinely strong. If you skipped the Season Pass thinking you’d be fine without them, you’re now 15-20% behind optimized Sinking teams. It’s not insurmountable, but it’s noticeable. The first exclusive gives +12% damage to Sinking applicators. The second gives +18% damage scaling per enemy Sinking stack. You’re stacking two damage buffs that add multiplicatively. Skip them, and you’re hitting with 85% of the damage of someone who bought them. That’s the difference between clearing in 5 turns and grinding out 6-7.
The Hot Take
Here’s my unpopular opinion: Poise is overrated. I know. Pinky Apprentice Sinclair is phenomenal. W Corp has great IDs. The problem is Poise teams take 6-8 turns to scale, and Sinking teams kill things in 4-5 turns. By the time your Poise build’s damage goes stupid, the encounter’s already over. Poise shines in Refraction Railway and post-game content where enemies have 20K+ HP. For Canto IX? Sinking is objectively faster.
Final Composition Recommendations
Your goal: speed
Run Sinking if you’ve got:
Heathcliff (any decent ID, WildHunt preferred)
Outis (W Corp L3 specifically)
Sinclair (House of Spiders: Pinky Apprentice)
If you don’t: build Bleed using Manager Don as your center piece.
If you’ve got neither: level whatever sinners you have and build a mixed comp focusing on single high-damage hitter rather than spreading damage thin across six identities.
Your goal: survival
This means you’re probably undergeared for content. Add a healer-type sinner (Faust has some healing combo potential) and reduce your damage slots from 4-5 down to 3. You won’t clear faster, but you’ll actually finish.
Your goal: making resources count
Don’t build for Tremor, Rupture, or mixed-effect teams. Seriously. Pick one damage type and commit. Your 100 resources go further when concentrated than spread thin.
Season 7 is good. Really good. The meta makes sense, the content’s challenging without being unfair, and the team-building rewards specialization. Sinking’s the move right now. Build it. Deploy it. Optimize it. You’ll thank yourself when you’re clearing Canto IX encounters in 4-5 turns instead of grinding them down over 8-10.
See you out there.
